🟢 Slide — Class diagram
Script (~1 min 10s)

"This is the class diagram — the data structure of the platform.
At the top, the class User holds the common attributes: id, name, email and password, and the method login(). Two classes inherit from User: the Student, who adds a currentLevel and the actions takeQuiz(), askTutor() and followPath(); and the Administrator, who adds the management operations — manageContent(), manageUsers(), manageAIModels() and viewStatistics(). I used inheritance because both share the same identity but behave differently.
On the right is the content side. A Course is composed of several Lessons and several Quizzes; each Quiz is composed of several Questions. I used composition — the filled diamond — because a lesson, a quiz or a question cannot exist without its parent.
On the left is the learning activity. A Student has many Attempts — one for each quiz they take — and each Attempt belongs to one Quiz. A Student also has many Mastery records: one per concept, with a level value. This Mastery class is exactly where my adaptive engine, Bayesian Knowledge Tracing, stores the estimated level of the student for each concept.
The numbers on the links are the multiplicities: for example, one student has zero to many attempts, and many attempts belong to one quiz."
Why we did it this way (if the jury asks)

Why inheritance User → Student / Administrator? Both are users with the same login data, but with different behavior → generalization. It avoids duplicating id / name / email.
Why isn't role in User? Because the subclasses already encode the role — adding it too would be redundant.
Why composition (filled diamond) and not a simple association? Because there is a life-cycle dependency: if a course is deleted, its lessons, quizzes and questions no longer make sense → composition.
Why the Mastery class? It is the adaptive core: it stores the estimated level per concept (my BKT brick). This is what makes the path personalized.
Why a separate Attempt class? To keep the history of attempts (date, score) — useful for tracking and for updating mastery.
Why these multiplicities? They reflect reality: a student can retake a quiz several times (0..), a quiz always has at least one question (1..), and so on.
